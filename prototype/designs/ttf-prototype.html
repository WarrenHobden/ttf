Class B

Ketamine

Also called: Ket, K, Special K, Wonk, Vitamin K

A powerful anaesthetic used in human and animal medicine. Looks similar to cocaine, but is a very different drug.

Last reviewed: June 2026

At a glance

What it isA dissociative anaesthetic. A grainy white or light brown powder.
How it's takenUsually snorted ("a bump"). Sometimes injected into a muscle.
How long it lastsFelt in ~15 mins if snorted. The buzz lasts 30 mins–1 hour.
The lawClass B. Illegal to have, give away or sell.

Key risks & what to do now

  • Regular use can cause serious, sometimes permanent, bladder damage.
  • You don't feel pain properly, so you can badly injure yourself without realising.
  • Too much can put you in a "k-hole" — unable to move and feeling cut off from your body.
  • Mixing with alcohol, benzos or opioids can stop your breathing. This can be fatal.
If someone is unconscious, can't be woken, or is struggling to breathe: Call 999 What to do in an emergency

Mixing ketamine with other drugs

Mixing is always risky, but some combinations are far more dangerous than others. With ketamine, these can slow or stop your breathing:

+ Alcohol + Benzodiazepines + Opioids

How it feels

Ketamine is a dissociative drug — it produces a feeling of detachment from your body and the world around you. It can make people seem slower, more relaxed and chilled out, but it can also stop them moving properly or making sense.

How long the effects last depends on how much you've taken, your size, and what else you've taken. People may feel low in mood for a few days afterwards.

The risks in detail

  • Because you don't feel pain properly, you can injure yourself and not know it.
  • Taking too much can leave you unable to move and unaware of your surroundings (a "k-hole"), which can be very frightening.
  • Regular use is linked to serious bladder problems — some people need ongoing medical treatment, and damage can be permanent.
  • It's impossible to tell by looking whether ketamine has been cut with something else.
  • Never use alone — make sure people you trust know what you've taken and how much.

Ketamine is usually sold as a grainy white or light brown powder. It tastes bitter and unpleasant.

Most people who use powder ketamine snort it — taking a small amount is often called "a bump". Snorting is the most common way to take it in the UK. People who use it regularly sometimes inject it into a muscle for a bigger hit.

Yes. People who become addicted will keep taking it whether or not they're aware of the health risks. Regular use builds tolerance, which can lead to taking more to get the same effect.

There are no physical withdrawal symptoms, so ketamine addiction is sometimes called a psychological dependence. Drug treatment services can help people stop.

Ketamine is a Class B drug. That means it's illegal to have for yourself, give away or sell.

  • Possession can get you up to 5 years in prison, an unlimited fine, or both.
  • Supplying someone else — even your friends — can get you up to 14 years in prison, an unlimited fine, or both.

Home › Get help › What to do in an emergency

Call 999 NOW if they:

  • Are unconscious or won't wake up
  • Have difficulty breathing or have stopped
  • Have blue lips or fingertips
  • Are having a seizure (fit)
Call 999
Ambulance crews won't usually call the police. Your safety comes first.

What to tell the 999 operator

"I need an ambulance. My friend has taken drug name and they are describe symptoms. We are at your location."
If you carry naloxone, also tell them: "I have naloxone on me."

While you wait for the ambulance

1
Stay with them

Don't leave them alone, even for a moment

2
If they aren't breathing, start CPR

The 999 operator will talk you through it

3
If they're awake, keep them calm

Speak softly, don't shout or shake them

4
If you have naloxone and think it's an opioid overdose, use it

Follow the instructions on the kit and tell the crew you've given it

5
If they're unconscious but breathing: recovery position

Lie them on their side so they can breathe

6
Gather any drugs left over

Give them to the ambulance crew — it helps treatment

What to do in specific situations

Opioids include heroin, methadone, some prescription painkillers and nitazenes. Signs of an overdose:

  • Very slow or stopped breathing
  • Blue or grey lips or fingertips
  • Can't be woken
  • Very small "pinpoint" pupils

Call 999. If you have naloxone, use it now — it reverses an opioid overdose. If they aren't breathing, start CPR.

  • Move them to a quiet, calm space
  • Keep them away from bright lights and loud noise
  • Speak calmly and reassuringly
  • Encourage slow, deep breaths
  • Stay with them until they feel better
  • Move them somewhere cool
  • Remove excess clothing
  • Sip water slowly (not too fast)
  • Fan them or use a cool cloth
  • Call 999 if their temperature doesn't drop
  • Keep them sitting up if possible
  • Talk to them to keep them awake
  • Don't give them coffee
  • Don't put them in a cold bath
  • If you can't wake them: call 999 immediately

Recovery position

Use this if someone is unconscious but breathing.

Tilt their head back and lift their chin to open the airway

Lie them on their side with their legs straight

Place their nearest arm at right angles to their body

Pull the far leg up and roll them towards you

Keep their head tilted back so the airway stays open

Recovery position — keeps the airway clear if they vomit

After the emergency

It's normal to feel shaky, anxious or drained after helping someone. Here's what to expect:

  • You may feel anxious for a few hours — this is normal and will pass
  • Drink water and eat something when you feel able
  • Talk to someone about what happened — don't bottle it up
  • Rest when you can — your body needs time to recover too
